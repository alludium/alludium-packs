Evaluation Workstream Guide

Guide for lightweight commercial, technical, financial, and team evaluation before formal diligence.

Purpose

Use this guide during the evaluation stage, after screening and before formal diligence. The goal is to test the opportunity's main investment hypotheses, identify gating risks, and decide what evidence is needed for decision review.

Evaluation is not formal diligence. It should be fast, evidence-led, and explicit about uncertainty.

Evaluation Versus Formal Diligence

DimensionEvaluationFormal Diligence
TimingBefore decision review and term-sheet workAfter decision review, term-sheet direction, or formal diligence approval
GoalDecide whether the opportunity deserves more workVerify claims and uncover blockers before investment
InputsPitch materials, public research, calls, early data, initial model, customer or expert snippetsData room, company-provided files, references, legal/counsel inputs, expert review, detailed models
OutputCurrent posture, central thesis question, gating risks, evidence gaps, meeting/data-room planWorkstream report, verification status, risk register, counsel/expert questions, approval conditions
Evidence standardGood enough to route the opportunityStrong enough to support investment decision conditions

Workstream Outputs

Every evaluation workstream should produce:

OutputMeaning
Current viewPositive, mixed, negative, or unknown.
Main hypothesisThe specific belief being tested.
Evidence reviewedSources used, with confidence and recency.
Company claimsClaims not yet independently verified.
Gating riskThe risk most likely to change the investment posture.
Next proof neededArtifact, meeting, reference, model, expert call, or legal question.
Decision impactPass, watch, targeted diligence, decision review, or term-sheet consideration.

Commercial Evaluation

Focus on market, customer, GTM, pricing, competition, and category timing. A good commercial evaluation answers whether a specific customer segment has urgent pain, budget, and a credible path to adoption.

Do not require formal customer references unless the opportunity has reached formal diligence. Instead, identify which customer, partner, pipeline, or market evidence would be needed next.

Technical Evaluation

Focus on product depth, technical edge, architecture plausibility, IP or data defensibility, roadmap realism, and technical team coverage. A good technical evaluation identifies which technical claims must be proved before decision review or formal diligence.

Do not imply code review, security review, expert validation, or IP clearance has happened unless those inputs were actually reviewed.

Financial Evaluation

Focus on forecast plausibility, burn, runway, use of funds, financing path, valuation, ownership, and return sensitivity. A good financial evaluation identifies whether the financing plan can plausibly produce the next-stage proof needed by the company and the fund.

Do not treat forecast models as verified. Flag missing source models, untested assumptions, unsupported customer-count forecasts, and valuation/ownership risks.

Team Evaluation

Focus on founder-market fit, role coverage, relevant experience, ambition, integrity signals, hiring gaps, board/adviser quality, and coachability. A good team evaluation identifies whether the team can execute the critical success factors and where formal references or deeper review are needed.

Iteration Standard

Evaluation workstreams may run more than once. Each rerun should state what changed since the prior evaluation and which new evidence changed, confirmed, or weakened the view.

Standard

End with a concrete recommendation and the next evidence request. Do not convert incomplete evaluation into formal diligence language.
